Yung, Brandon, DES 157 In Chapter 13 of “Don’t Make Me Think,” Steve Krug explains the differences between usability and user centered design, and the significance of incorporating the principles behind user centered research into an environment where usability is not supported.

“Usability” and “User Centered Design,” or UCD were terms that people used to describe any efforts to design with the user in mind. Usability and Information Architecture were the primary professions focused on Web site usability, but now we mostly hear User Experience Design, or UX. UCD focuses on the whole product’s usability, while UX considers the users’ needs at every stage of the product life cycle. To build support for usability in the workplace, we should relate it to the company’s Return of Investment, and also speaking in terms of relevant issues for the company. There are five ways that you can convince the office to support usability, which include getting your boss to watch a usability test, do the first test on your own time, test out the competition, empathize with management, and know your place in the grand scheme of things. We should never try to focus on users’ desires, as this often links to deception and manipulation. Evernote incorporated user-testing to increase user retention by 15 percent, specifically by understanding customer interactions and streamlining the sign up process.

Although Krug offers solid points for incorporating usability into the production stage, I believe there should be more clarity on what makes a product “better.” Everyone uses applications and websites differently, so what makes something better is subjective but Krug makes it sound like there is only one right answer. All in all, Krug’s guides are only meant to be guides, and so he advises to push the constraints if better for the user.

Blog Post #1 1/12/17

I ultimately chose Reddit as my choice of interface design to evaluate. The reason I chose Reddit was because Reddit is one of the most popular interfaces browsed around the world, and it features many different types of users. However, all users have one purpose on the site – they seek information and so they want to learn.


Design: The design of Reddit is straightforward, though it is definitely outdated. The white background is meant to enable users to read text easily, and the lack of imagery and animations in general enables users to load the page as quickly as possible. This goes hand-in-hand with Reddit being a text-heavy site. Users come to the site to converse with each other, read each other’s comments, and write their own. The consistency and the spacing of the website make the site very easy to use.


Appeal: To put it bluntly, Reddit relies on its content for appeal to users, not its aesthetic appeal. Nevertheless, thousands of users visit the site every hour to refresh themselves on the news and to read each other’s opinions on countless topics.


Effectiveness: Ultimately, type hierarchy and consistent spacing are the main reasons why Reddit is able to function as a heavy-duty website. However, I would not rate it as ‘effective’ as it is easy to drown oneself in all of the content that it has to offer, as there are nearly a hundred clickable links but only 3 different colors for the links.
